\section{Introduction and Features}

Charles Hill's original Pocket FT8 project\cite{pocketFT8} has inspired several\cite{dxFT8}\cite{tinyDX} fully self-contained FT8 transceiver derivatives in which the computer \textit{is} the radio.
This derivative revisits Charles' original concept with simplified hardware and enhanced firmware features while retaining the highly portable, single-band, QRPP, HF transceiver concept for backpacking and day hike applications.
The features include:

  \begin{itemize} [noitemsep]
  \item Open source hardware and firmware
  \item Self-contained and light-weight
  \item Compact, 4-layer PCB (4.0X2.8")
  \item FT8 modulation
  \item 275+ mW RF output (QRPP)
  \item Single RF filter slot
  \item Low jitter TCXO
  \item GPS disciplined date, time and location (grid square)
  \item 480x320 pixel TFT calibrated touchscreen
  \item Waterfall traffic display
  \item FT8 QSO sequencer
  \item Automatic QSO logging to an  ADIF file
  \item JSON station configuration file
  \item Operation from a single 5V USB power block
  \end{itemize}
 
The FT8 protocol\cite{qexFT8}, designed for weak signal communication,  reduces the transceiver's power requirements, weight, and replaces the telegraph key, microphone, and headset accessory with a touchscreen display.
Consolidating the FT8 radio and its touchscreen with an embedded computer eliminates the need to pack a computer in addition to the radio.
Further integration of a GPS receiver provides the UTC date and time for ADIF logging as well as the Maidenhead grid square and FT8 time-synchronization.
On the other hand, FT8 QSOs are usually terse, contest-like exchanges conveying a sequence of structured messages with minimal operator involvement following establishment of a contact.  
While FT8 is not always appropriate, alternative designs\cite{kh1}\cite{qmx}  cover many of those situations.
  
 \section{Hardware}
The transceiver (Figure \ref{fig:schematic}) is designed around a Teensy 4.1 600 mHz, 32-bit ARM Cortex-M7 MCU with hardware floating point, USB, SPI, I2C, 1 mB of RAM, and two ADCs.

When transmitting, the MCU reprograms the Si5351 clock generator's output for each of the eight FT8 Frequency Shift Keying (FSK) tones, a direct FSK scheme, unlike the audio FSK (aFSK) widely used with a SSB transmitters' microphone jack connected from a laptop headphone circuit.
Frequency stability is achieved by locking the Si5351 to a low jitter Temperature Controlled Crystal Oscillator (TCXO) reference.
The Si5351's output is buffered by one element of a SN74ACT244 octal bus driver whose remaining seven buffers function in parallel as the transmitter's power amplifier (PA).
A five-pole Chebyshev low-pass filter (Figure \ref{fig:lpFilter}) with 1 dB of ripple peaking near the expected operating frequency  provides harmonic rejection.
The PA delivers 275 mW through the filter.

The Si4735 implements the receiver's RF section and delivers FT8 audio tones to the MCU's audio pipeline via ADC2.
While the Si4735 may lack the performance of alternative designs, its capabilities compare to the QRPP transmitter:  on-the-air experience has been the receiver decodes more stations than respond.
Early prototypes resolved the Si4735's sensitivity to noise on its I2C bus by moving other I2C peripherals to a different bus.
The Si4735's phase-locked loop (PLL) is locked to one of the Si5351 clock generator's outputs.

An Adafruit 3.5" TFT 320x480 touchscreen with an HX8357D controller serve as the graphical user interface device.
The display achieves satisfactory performance over an SPI bus from the MCU while the resistive touchscreen connects to the MCU's ADC1.
While the compact display is ideal for portable operation, accurate interaction with the touchscreen favors use of a stylus.








 
 \subsection*{FL1 Design}
FL1 mainly provides harmonic rejection for the transmitted signal.
On 40 meters, the five pole Chebyshev design provides for 1 dB of ripple peaking inside the band.

During receive, the Chebyshev low-pass design offers only minimal rejection of out-of-band signals. 
While this has not proven to be a significant issue in the wilderness use-case, the receiver might benefit from a bandpass design in other applications.
